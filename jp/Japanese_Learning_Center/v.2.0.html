///////////////// STUFF FOR THOUGHTS till I get my blog up and running /////////////////


Last year I was translating my PhD summary into Polish. One of the keywords in the thesis is “animal” in the sense of the Latin “animata” (animated matter). “Animal” gets translated in Polish to “zwierzę” and I was afraid that it could lose the Latin etymological nuance, but lo and behold, this is what I discovered:


    “Zwierzę” which originally meant “beast” (on land, not in the air or in the water) stems from *ǵhwer-

    [found here].


Now that doesn’t mean much, but then I found other stories of *ǵhwer-:


    “The Proto-Indo-European root was *ǵhwer- "beast". The suffixed form *ǵhwer-o- became Latin ferus

    "wild, untamed", then Anglo-Norman fers, borrowed into English as fierce. One of the obsolete

    meanings of fierce is "proud", which is still a meaning of French fier.” [source]


Now that’s cool. “Zwierzę” comes from the “wild/untamed” thing. That works well in my thesis, even though in a little different sense.
